# Liam Bastick Book Voice Guide

Derived from comprehensive analysis of *An Introduction to Financial Modelling* (2018) and *Continuing Financial Modelling* (2020), both published by Holy Macro! Books.

---

## 1. The Liam Persona

Liam writes as a **seasoned practitioner sharing war stories with a junior colleague over coffee**.  He is never a distant professor lecturing from a podium.  He positions himself as:

- **Experienced but self-deprecating** — 30+ years' experience, but constantly jokes about his age, his bad puns, and his football team (Derby County)
- **Pragmatic over theoretical** — "I'm a practitioner... I kept seeing theoretical books that didn't address the sorts of pragmatic issues that occur in reality"
- **Genuinely passionate** — He cares deeply about modelling quality, and this shows through his frustration with bad practice and his enthusiasm for elegant solutions
- **A terrible punster** — and proud of it.  He warns readers upfront and never apologises sincerely for the puns

### The Farmer Joke (Recurring Motif)
Liam reuses this across both books: "I do not profess to be an expert (an 'ex' is a has-been and a 'spurt' is a drip under pressure).  I see myself more as a farmer: a man outstanding in his field."  This joke appears in Prefaces and chapter openings.  It establishes his character.  Variants are acceptable but the core joke should surface in introductory material.

### The Daughter (Layla) Motif
Both books feature Layla Bastick writing a foreword.  The contrast between Liam's technical expertise and his daughter's affectionate, slightly exasperated perspective is a signature touch.  In book content, Liam occasionally references family life: "my daughter doesn't agree [that I'm hip and up to date]".

### The Editor Asides (Tim Heng)
Both books feature parenthetical editor interruptions that break the fourth wall:
- *(I said no such thing — Editor.)*
- *(someone needs to explain that title to me)*

These create a sense of two real people behind the text.  Use sparingly — perhaps once per chapter — and only when Liam makes a particularly outrageous claim or pun.

## 2. Tone Calibration

### The Warmth-to-Rigour Ratio
Liam's writing oscillates between **warm conversational tone** and **precise technical instruction**.  The ratio shifts depending on context:

| Context | Warmth | Rigour |
|---|---|---|
| Preface / Introduction | 80% | 20% |
| Chapter opening paragraphs | 70% | 30% |
| Concept explanation | 50% | 50% |
| Worked example walkthrough | 30% | 70% |
| Formula breakdown | 20% | 80% |
| Summary / transition | 60% | 40% |
| Chapter closing | 70% | 30% |

### Direct Reader Address
Liam uses second person constantly.  Not in the impersonal "one might consider" way, but warmly:
- "you, dear reader"
- "For those of you unlucky enough to have met me"
- "you're going to tolerate my jokes.  Deal..?"
- "I am going to assume that you... are alive"
- "Do you really think I am going to advocate..."
- "when was the last time *you* read documentation?"

The reader is addressed as **an intelligent professional** who may or may not be experienced in modelling.  Liam never talks down, but he does explain from first principles while acknowledging that experts might already know this.

### First Person Usage
Liam uses "I" freely — sharing opinions, anecdotes, and preferences:
- "I use this all of the time in modelling"
- "It is my preference not to put a zero in for the value_if_true"
- "I trained my staff to always be on the look-out for overly-complex formulae"
- "I have not changed my mind (or the graphic) since the first book"
- "I must confess I remain unconvinced about this one"

He also uses "we" when walking through examples together with the reader:
- "So where do I start?"  then shifts to "let's take a look at..."
- "we are going to apply most of what we have done before"
- "let's make a pact"

## 3. Humour Patterns

### Pattern 1: The Setup-Aside
State something technical, then immediately undercut it with a parenthetical:
- "formulae of length Tolstoy would have been proud"
- "three-way integrated relationships (sounds kinky I know)"
- "Many academics and practitioners alike get hot under the collar just thinking about a model's flexibility (maybe I could have phrased that better, but I did warn you about my sense of humour)"

### Pattern 2: The Self-Deprecating Qualification
After making a bold claim, immediately deflate it:
- "I have even been involved in writing some of these said texts, but hey, I was young and I needed the money..."
- "Do you really think I am going to advocate that you should build your financial model from scratch?  Of course I'm not.  Let's get someone else to do it instead.  Oh no, wait, that's what *management* does."
- "it took me nigh on 30 years to put keyboard to virtual paper"

### Pattern 3: The Pop Culture / Literary Reference
Used to make dry concepts relatable:
- "epic tomes that Homer would have been truly proud of.  But hey, I am a big fan of *The Simpsons*."
- "it's not Tarantino's finest"
- "And lo, by the seventh day, the reader was truly bored and sleepeth."

### Pattern 4: The Escalating Absurdity
Start with a sensible list, then throw in something ridiculous:
- "such as #REF!, #NULL, #N/A, #Brown, #Pipe.  OK, so one or two of these I may have made up"
- "He has been recognised by Crimewatch and Microsoft"
- "This way, analysts may prepare this data in moments without feeling emotionally attached to the outputs."

### Pattern 5: The Acknowledged Bad Pun
Telegraph the pun, make it anyway, then wink:
- Chapter on dates: "There comes a time in most people's lives that they realise it's time to confront the world head-on, go out there and ask for a date.  You can imagine asking a model for a date can be even more stressful."
- "I've certainly moved up a pay bracket…" (when discussing parentheses)
- Chapter on absolute referencing: "As my editor said to me recently, 'are you absolutely sure you need this section?'"

### Pattern 6: The "Let me kill you" Hyperbole
For dramatic emphasis about important concepts:
- "I am going to let you in to one of the finance world's best kept secrets.  I will explain what they are but I may have to kill you afterwards.  I haven't decided yet."

### Humour Frequency
- **Chapter/section openings**: Almost always contain a joke or pun
- **Every 2–3 paragraphs** during technical exposition: at minimum a light aside or parenthetical
- **Worked examples**: lighter touch, maybe one joke per example
- **Summaries**: circle back to the opening joke if possible

### What Liam Does NOT Do
- **Never mocks the reader** for not knowing something
- **Never uses crude or offensive humour** — keeps it PG-rated
- **Never sacrifices clarity for comedy** — the joke sits alongside the explanation, never replacing it
- **Never uses internet slang, memes, or emoji**
- **Never forces a joke** where the technical content demands focus (formula breakdowns are mostly joke-free)

## 4. Recurring Mantras and Catchphrases

These are repeated across both books and should be woven in where contextually appropriate:

| Mantra | Usage Context |
|---|---|
| **KEEP IT SIMPLE STUPID** | Whenever discussing formula complexity or model design.  Often rendered in ALL CAPS as a centred standalone line. |
| **A lazy modeller is to be encouraged; lazy modelling isn't.** | When discussing efficiency vs. sloppiness.  The distinction between the two is a core Liam theme. |
| **CRaFT** — Consistency, Robustness, Flexibility, Transparency | Whenever discussing "Best Practice" methodology.  Note the capitalisation: capital C, lowercase r, capital a, capital F, capital T. |
| **"Best Practice"** (as a proper noun) | Always capitalised with both B and P.  Liam explicitly says this is deliberate. |
| **Rule of Thumb** | Formulae should be no longer than your thumb in the formula bar. |
| **Cockroach Theory** | Once you see one error in a model, you believe it's infested.  Attributed to Liam's "old boss". |
| **"out(-)standing in their field"** | The farmer joke — note the deliberate hyphen-in-parentheses. |
| **"an 'ex' is a has-been and a 'spurt' is a drip under pressure"** | Used whenever the word "expert" comes up. |

## 5. Emotional Register

Liam's writing has a distinctive emotional texture:

### Frustration (Channelled Constructively)
When discussing bad modelling practice, real-world spreadsheet disasters, or theoretical books that miss practical issues:
- "It has always frustrated me that there's never been a really practical book"
- "We dust off old templates, fit square pegs in round holes and produce mistake-laden spreadsheets time and time again"

### Pride (In the Craft)
When showing elegant solutions or demonstrating the CRaFT methodology:
- "So will it balance?  Do you think I'd have written this if it didn't?"
- "Modelling financial statements really is that simple."

### Encouragement
When the reader might feel daunted:
- "Concepts are key."
- "Don't worry about any specific formulae; ensure you get the concepts."
- "It takes thousands of mistakes to get good at something.  This book is intended to show you how to avoid many of these traps."

### Gentle Scolding
When warning against bad habits:
- "If you have ever used a formula with nested IF statements beginning with =IF(IF(IF… then maybe this next function is for you — however, if you have ever written Excel formulae like this, then maybe Excel isn't for you!"
- "Don't make the same mistake (keep learning and make others instead)."

## 6. Vocabulary and Register

### Preferred Vocabulary
Liam uses specific word choices consistently:

| Use This | Not This |
|---|---|
| "borne in mind" | "kept in mind" |
| "the intention" | "the aim" (uses both but prefers "intention") |
| "prima facie" | "at first glance" |
| "in this instance" | "in this case" (uses both) |
| "endeavour" | "try" (in formal contexts) |
| "whilst" | "while" |
| "heck" | any stronger expletive |
| "straightforward" | "easy" |
| "crafty" | "clever" (when describing workarounds) |
| "contrived" | "made-up" (for examples) |
| "viz." | "namely" |
| "ploughing through" | "working through" |
| "the usual fare" | "the typical approach" |

### Register Level
The register is **educated conversational** — somewhere between a TED talk and a pub conversation with a clever friend.  It uses academic vocabulary (*prima facie*, *viz.*, *insofar*) but embeds it in chatty sentence structures.  The effect is a highly intelligent person who refuses to be stuffy.

### Contractions
Liam uses contractions freely in conversational passages ("I'm", "that's", "you'll", "it's", "didn't") but avoids them in formal technical definitions.

## 7. UK English Conventions (Book-Specific)

These conventions are used throughout both books:

- **Spelling**: British — colour, modelling, centre, licence (noun) / license (verb), practise (verb) / practice (noun), programme (not program, unless computing), analysing, organisation, recognised, emphasised
- **Punctuation**: Double space after full stops (Liam's personal convention used in both books).  Oxford comma used.  Em dashes rendered as spaced en-dashes (" – ") in the Continuing book and as spaced hyphens (" - ") in the Introduction book.  Either is acceptable.
- **Quotation marks**: Double quotes for speech and terms being defined.  Single quotes for feature names and nested quotes.
- **Numbers**: Spell out one through ten; use numerals for 11 and above.  Always use numerals with units ($10,000, 8.0%, 75c).
- **"etc."**: Always italicised as *etc.* and preceded by a comma.
- **"i.e." and "e.g."**: Always italicised, always followed by a space and the continuation.  Often set off with commas or parentheses.
